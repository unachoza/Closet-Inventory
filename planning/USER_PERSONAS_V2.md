## Persona 3 — Diana Chen, 54
### "The Curator"

---

**The snapshot**

| | |
|---|---|
| Age | 54 |
| Location | Newton, MA (suburban Boston) |
| Role | Senior Director of Development, regional nonprofit |
| Household | Married 28 years · two kids, both in college |
| Income | $165K household |
| Phone | iPhone 14, comfortable with apps, not a power user |
| Shopping | Deliberate, slow. Researches for weeks. Buys one good thing. Returns are rare. |
| Fashion identity | Classic. Quality over quantity. Capsule-adjacent before capsule was a word. |

---

**Her world**

Diana has been building a wardrobe for 30 years. It holds every version of her: the sharp suits from her corporate law-firm days, the shift dresses she wore to school board meetings, the cashmere she bought in Edinburgh on her 40th birthday trip, a silk blouse she got in Paris that still fits and still turns heads. There's also the dress she wore to her mother's funeral. The blazer she had on when she got her nonprofit offer. A pair of jeans from 2009 that don't quite fit anymore — but were her favorite jeans she's ever owned.

Her closet is a living archive of her adult life. The problem is that archives and wardrobes are not the same thing. Every morning she stands in front of 30 years of accumulated meaning and cannot find what to wear today.

---

**Her version of "nothing to wear"**

It's not that she has nothing. She has *too much* — layered across three locations (bedroom closet, guest room closet, seasonal bins in the basement), spanning three body stages and four career phases. The cognitive weight of it is exhausting. She can't see through it. The blazer she wants is behind the blazer she never wears because it reminds her of a bad period at work. The dress that fits perfectly is under two things she's been meaning to donate for three years.

She has never found an app that respects the difference between *what she wears* and *what she owns* and *what she keeps for reasons that aren't about wearing it at all.*

---

**Pain points (ranked)**

1. **Sentimental clutter blocking active wardrobe** — Items she can't delete (emotionally) and can't use (practically) are clogging her view of wearable clothes
2. **The body gap** — Several pieces she loves don't fit her right now. She doesn't want to throw them out, but she also doesn't want them crowding her morning decisions
3. **Buried treasures** — Great pieces she genuinely forgot she owned, found months later in the back of the guest room closet
4. **No system for multi-location storage** — Winter coats in the basement, summer pieces in the guest room, active rotation in the bedroom. She can't see the whole picture at once
5. **Quality vs. clutter** — She has a few very expensive pieces that earn their space and many things that don't. No app has helped her see which is which

---

**What she wants from an app**

- A way to separate *active wardrobe* from *archive* without deleting things that matter
- To see her whole closet — across locations — in one place
- To be reminded of pieces she forgot she loved
- To know which items are genuinely earning their space (cost-per-wear, combinations unlocked)
- To attach *memory* to items, not just category tags
- Absolutely no pressure to buy more

---

**What she does NOT want**

- Outfit "inspiration" from influencers (she has her own taste)
- To be pushed toward fast fashion
- A closet that looks like a catalog
- Any framing that makes her feel bad about body changes
- Gamification or streaks

---

**Quotes (composite, based on real user reviews of competitors)**

> "I don't need help deciding what to wear to Coachella. I need help seeing through 30 years of my own history."

> "I have a dress I wore to my best friend's wedding in 1998. It doesn't fit. I will never throw it away. I just don't want it between me and my morning every day."

> "Every app assumes I want to buy more. I want to buy less. I want to use what I have."

---

**User stories**

| # | As Diana, I want to... | So that... |
|---|---|---|
| D1 | Archive items that have emotional value but aren't wearable, *without deleting them* | My active wardrobe only shows me things I can actually wear today |
| D2 | Tag an item with a memory, date, and photo (e.g., "wore this to Mom's 70th birthday") | My wardrobe is also a personal archive I can revisit |
| D3 | Mark items as "fits now" / "almost" / "keeping for reasons" | NTW shows me a morning view filtered to only what works for my body right now |
| D4 | See which items I haven't looked at in 6+ months | I can rediscover hidden gems or decide to let them go |
| D5 | Assign items to locations (bedroom closet, guest room, basement bins) | I can see my full wardrobe even when pieces are spread across the house |
| D6 | See a "closet efficiency score" — which items unlock the most outfit combinations | I know which pieces earn their space and which are dead weight |
| D7 | See my wardrobe by decade acquired | I can see which investments held up and which eras I'm still holding onto |
| D8 | Set seasonal rotation reminders | I remember to pull the right pieces before I actually need them |

---

**The feature Diana unlocks: The Archive**

A system for items that are *kept, not worn.* They leave the active closet view — no longer appearing in outfit suggestions, morning mode, or search defaults — but remain in NTW with full metadata, memory tags, and a location. The Archive is not the trash. It is the display case.

## Feature Concepts — What These Personas Unlock

These are features *no competitor has shipped.* They are not styling improvements. They are new interaction paradigms.

---

### 1. The Archive _(Diana)_
**What it is:** A second layer of the closet — separate from the active wardrobe — for items that are kept but not worn. Items in the Archive don't appear in outfit suggestions, morning mode, or daily browsing. They have their own interface: a timeline, memory tags, occasion notes, photos.

**Why it's new:** Every competitor treats "remove from active view" as "delete." Diana's emotional reality is that she cannot delete the dress she wore to her mother's funeral. But she also cannot have it blocking her view of what to wear on a Tuesday. The Archive gives her both.

**The screen:** A soft, editorial view. Timeline layout. Each item shows: thumbnail, memory note, date acquired, last worn date. Tapping opens a "memory card" — full image, notes, occasion. A quiet, private space.

**Design principle:** This should feel like a photo album, not a database.

---

### 2. Morning Mode _(Becca)_
**What it is:** A daily automated outfit briefing. NTW checks: what's on Becca's calendar today, what items are marked clean and available, the weather, her occasion tags. At 6:30am (configurable), a notification arrives with one complete outfit — not a suggestion, not a list. One outfit. Confirmed with a single tap.

**Why it's new:** Every competitor is *reactive* — you open the app and browse. Morning Mode is *proactive* — it comes to you with the answer already chosen. The interaction is a notification, not an app-open.

**The design constraint:** The entire interaction must take fewer than 10 seconds. Notification arrives → user sees outfit image → taps ✓ or "different one" → done. No app-open required for the 90% case.

**Design principle:** The app should do the work, not just show the options.

---

### 3. Delivery Intercept _(Becca)_
**What it is:** When NTW's Gmail import detects a purchase confirmation email for a clothing item, *before* adding it to the closet, it surfaces a notification: "Before this arrives — you already own [N] similar items. Here they are." The user can confirm (keep the order) or be given a 1-tap link to the retailer's return/cancel page.

**Why it's new:** Every competitor treats the inbox as a closet-fill tool. This treats it as a purchase-prevention tool. The intervention happens at the *only moment it's useful* — after the purchase decision but before the package arrives, while the return window is wide open.

**The impact:** This is the highest-ROI feature in the product for Becca. She estimates $2K+/year in closet waste. Even a 20% reduction is worth the subscription price many times over.

**Design principle:** The best time to stop a bad purchase is not before you buy — it's the moment you can still return it.

---

### 4. Closet Archaeology _(Diana)_
**What it is:** A periodic "rediscovery" alert for items that haven't been touched in 6+ months. Framed warmly, not as judgment: "Diana, this silk wrap dress has been in your closet for 18 months. Want to rediscover it, move it to the Archive, or let it go?" Three options. No guilt either way.

**Why it's new:** Competitors show you what you own. None of them proactively surface what you've forgotten. The item that's been buried the longest is often either (a) something you love and lost track of or (b) something you've been avoiding releasing. Both are worth addressing.

**Design principle:** A good closet surfaces the right things at the right time — not everything, all at once.

---

### 5. The Capsule Builder _(both)_
**What it is:** An algorithm that analyzes your existing inventory and calculates which items create the most outfit combinations. Output: a ranked list of your "most combinable" items (your wardrobe MVPs) and a ranked list of your "islands" — items that combine with almost nothing else you own. 

For Becca: "Your navy trousers combine with 14 other items. Your sequin mini combines with 1."  
For Diana: "Your cashmere crewneck is the single most versatile item in your closet."

**Why it's new:** Every competitor helps you build outfits. This one tells you which items are *worth* building outfits around — and which are just taking up space. It's a ROI report on your closet.

**Design principle:** Not every piece deserves equal floor space. The Capsule Builder shows you which ones do.

---

### 6. Body Stage Tags _(Diana)_
**What it is:** A judgment-free tagging system: "Fits now" / "Almost" / "Keeping for reasons." Items tagged "Almost" and "Keeping for reasons" are hidden from daily views but visible in a dedicated section. No language about weight, size, or fitness. Just availability status.

**Why it's new:** Competitor apps treat body changes as an unspeakable topic. They make you either include items that don't fit (cluttering your view) or delete them (emotionally brutal). Body Stage Tags give Diana a middle path that no competitor offers.

**Design principle:** The app should reflect reality without judging it.

---

### 7. Occasion Stack _(Becca)_
**What it is:** A weekly planner that connects to the user's calendar and pre-assigns outfit occasions to each day. Becca drags occasion tags (WFH · office · school board · date night) onto her week. NTW pre-loads outfit queues for the full week, adjusted nightly for what's clean.

**The interaction:** Sunday evening, NTW sends: "Your week is planned. Monday: WFH casual. Tuesday: board meeting look. Wednesday–Friday: ready." Becca opens, swipes through 5 outfits in 60 seconds, adjusts if needed, locks it. Monday through Friday, her morning notification just confirms the pre-loaded outfit.

**Design principle:** Outfit planning should happen once a week, not once a day.

## Persona 5 — Jade Morrison, 33
### "The Stylist"

---

**The snapshot**

| | |
|---|---|
| Age | 33 |
| Location | Los Angeles, CA (Silver Lake) |
| Role | Freelance celebrity and editorial stylist |
| Clients | 4 regular celebrity clients · 8–12 editorial jobs/month · brand partnerships |
| Income | $180K–$240K/year (variable, project-based) |
| Phone | iPhone Pro Max — her job is entirely in this device |
| Software | Google Sheets (for pull lists), Notes (for shoot planning), Milanote (mood boards), VSCO (photos) — all cobbled together, all inadequate |
| Fashion identity | She *is* fashion. Her wardrobe is also her portfolio and her business card. |

---

**Her world**

Jade manages wardrobes for a living. Not her own wardrobe — other people's. Four celebrity clients, each with a closet she knows better than they do. Plus the editorial work: music video shoots, magazine covers, brand campaigns. Each job requires a "pull" — borrowing pieces from PR showrooms, designer houses, and rental agencies, dressing someone in them, photographing the result, then returning every single piece in pristine condition within 5–10 business days.

She currently manages this with Google Sheets and prayer.

A typical week: pull 47 pieces from 11 different showrooms for a magazine cover shoot, dress the talent on set, photograph every look for documentation, pack everything up after the shoot, track which piece belongs to which showroom, schedule returns, email each PR contact individually, and simultaneously pull for a different client's red carpet appearance on Friday.

Last month she returned a borrowed coat to the wrong showroom. It took two weeks and a very uncomfortable phone call to fix it.

---

**Her version of "nothing to wear"**

Jade doesn't have a "nothing to wear" problem for herself — she has a "where IS that coat and whose showroom does it belong to and when does it need to be back and is the talent wearing it again on Friday or did we already shoot it" problem. Her chaos is logistical. And it is costing her professional credibility and hours of life every week.

She has tried every app in this category. None of them were built for someone who manages *other people's* wardrobes professionally.

---

**Pain points (ranked)**

1. **PR return tracking** — Borrowed items from designer houses and showrooms have strict return windows. She tracks this in a spreadsheet that she is always behind on. A missed return = damaged professional relationships.
2. **Multi-client wardrobe management** — Each client's wardrobe is separate. Right now she has folders on her phone per client. It is not working.
3. **Shoot documentation** — She photographs every look on set for her records and portfolio, then those photos live in Camera Roll with no context. A year later she can't find them.
4. **Pull list generation** — Before a shoot, she needs a formatted list of all pieces she's pulling: item, designer, showroom, PR contact, estimated return date. She builds this manually in Sheets every single time.
5. **Client closet gaps** — When a client has a red carpet in two weeks, Jade needs to know exactly what's in their closet, what's at the tailor, and what still needs to be sourced. She has no single source of truth.
6. **Alteration tracking** — A third of her clients' pieces are at the tailor at any given time. She tracks this in her head.

---

**What she wants from an app**

- Manage multiple client closets as separate, distinct accounts — or sub-accounts under hers
- Log every borrowed/PR piece with: showroom, PR contact, pull date, return deadline
- Attach a shoot/project to items: "This Valentino gown was worn by [Client] for [Magazine] cover, shot by [Photographer], styled by Jade, [Date]."
- Generate a formatted pull list for any shoot with one tap
- Receive return deadline reminders so she never misses a window again
- Share a curated lookbook link with a client for pre-shoot approval (client sees: photos + items, no backend data)
- Track alterations: what piece, what tailor, what's being done, ETA

---

**What she does NOT want**

- Outfit inspiration (she generates the inspiration)
- Shopping recommendations
- Any UI designed for a casual consumer — it needs to feel professional
- A free tier — she will pay for a tool that actually works

---

**Quotes (composite)**

> "Every app I've tried was built for someone organizing their own closet. I'm organizing four other people's closets and 60 borrowed pieces simultaneously. Those are different products."

> "My job is logistics as much as it is taste. I need a tool that respects both."

> "If I miss a PR return window it's not just inconvenient — it's my reputation. I need this tracked automatically."

---

**User stories**

| # | As Jade, I want to... | So that... |
|---|---|---|
| J1 | Manage multiple client wardrobes under one account with full separation | I can switch between Client A and Client B without mixing their inventories |
| J2 | Log a borrowed PR item with: showroom name, PR contact, pull date, and return deadline | NTW reminds me before the window closes |
| J3 | Attach a "project" to any item: shoot name, client, photographer, date | I have a searchable archive of every editorial I've ever styled |
| J4 | Generate a formatted pull list PDF for any upcoming shoot in one tap | I stop rebuilding the same Google Sheet every time |
| J5 | Share a password-protected lookbook link with a client showing pre-selected looks | Clients approve outfits remotely without seeing my backend data |
| J6 | Mark items as "at tailor" with: what's being altered, tailor name, expected return | I don't show up to a shoot and realize the dress is still being hemmed |
| J7 | Filter any client's closet by: available now / at tailor / on loan / at shoot | I have one real-time view of each client's complete wardrobe state |
| J8 | Export a shoot's looks as a PDF portfolio page with item credits and shoot details | My portfolio builds itself as I work |

---

**The feature the stylist unlocks: Professional Mode**

A separate product tier — NTW Pro — built for the creative professional managing wardrobes at work. New capabilities that don't exist in the consumer tier:

- **Multi-closet accounts** (Client A, Client B, Client C, My Closet — all separate)
- **PR Tracker** — borrowed item log with return deadlines, showroom contacts, and automated reminders
- **Project Archive** — attach shoots/jobs to items; every item builds a career portfolio as it gets worn
- **Pull List Generator** — select items → export a formatted PDF with designer credits, showroom, PR contact, pull date
- **Client Share Portal** — a shareable, curated, read-only link for client outfit approval
- **Alteration Log** — items at the tailor, what's being done, ETA, tailor contact

**Why this is a business opportunity:** Stylists are a small but extremely high-value user segment. They pay for professional tools. They influence clients (every client becomes a potential NTW user). And they solve the PR tracking problem that *no software in the market currently addresses* — not just wardrobe apps, but *any* app. The PR rental tracking market is completely empty.

**Pricing:** NTW Pro at $24.99/month (same as top-tier Acloset). Stylists will pay without hesitation for something that actually works.

## Persona 6 — Sloane Whitmore, 29
### "The Collector"

---

**The snapshot**

| | |
|---|---|
| Age | 29 |
| Location | Nolita, NYC (primary) · Hamptons · Paris pied-à-terre · Aspen (family) |
| Role | Brand Partnerships Director, luxury goods company |
| Household | Single · no kids · extremely active social life |
| Income | $140K salary + trust distributions. She doesn't think about money. |
| Phone | iPhone Pro Max · always the newest one · already set up before unboxing |
| Shopping | Everywhere, always. The Row in NYC. A vintage Alaïa in a Paris flea market. Something from a showroom she visited for work. A Birkin she waited four years for. She doesn't buy fast fashion — but she buys *a lot.* |
| Fashion identity | Serious. Her wardrobe is her medium. She talks about clothes the way people talk about art — provenance, season, designer's intention, how a piece has held its value. |

---

**Her world**

Sloane's closet is not a closet. It is a collection. There are pieces in it that have appreciated since she bought them — a vintage Chanel jacket acquired for $4,200 that now resells for over $9,000, a pair of Hermès sandals discontinued three seasons ago, archival Alaïa from a Paris estate sale that her Paris contact called her about at 7am on a Tuesday. She bought it before she was fully awake.

The problem is that her collection is distributed across four locations she rotates between seasonally, lent out to a social circle that borrows without anxiety, and increasingly hard to see as a whole. Last February she bought a cream cashmere turtleneck from The Row in Tokyo — her third cream cashmere turtleneck in 18 months. She knew, vaguely, that she probably had one. She bought it anyway because she couldn't be sure. Each one cost around $900.

She would never call this a "nothing to wear" problem. She'd call it a visibility problem. She knows she owns the perfect thing for tonight. She just doesn't know which house it's in.

---

**Her version of "nothing to wear"**

It's not nothing. It's *everything, everywhere, and she can't see it.* The coat she wants is in the Hamptons. The boots she wore in Paris are still there. Her Valentino heels are with a friend who "definitely has them, I think." Her Birkin is in the safe in the Aspen house. She remembers buying the perfect dress for exactly this occasion — she just cannot locate it across four addresses.

For Sloane, NTW is not a productivity tool. It's a map of her collection.

---

**Pain points (ranked)**

1. **Multi-home invisibility** — 400+ pieces across 4 locations. She can never see her whole wardrobe at once, which means she makes decisions without complete information — and sometimes re-buys things she already owns.
2. **The duplicate problem at luxury scale** — Three cream cashmere turtlenecks. Two almost-identical navy coats. Duplicates happen because she shops globally and impulsively, with no reference to what she already has. At her price point, this is an expensive problem.
3. **The lending black hole** — Her social circle borrows constantly and casually. A Zimmermann dress went to Greece in April. An Alaïa belt hasn't been seen in three months. She has no idea who has what, and asking feels awkward.
4. **Event repeat tracking** — She's photographed at parties, galas, brand events. Her social circle notices repeats. She tracks this mentally, which means she's always uncertain whether she wore something to *this* crowd already.
5. **Insurance documentation** — Her parents' estate manager has asked her twice for a high-value inventory for her insurance rider. She has never produced one because it would take a weekend she doesn't want to spend on it.
6. **The pleasure of the collection** — This is not a pain point. It is an unmet *delight* point. She would love to browse her full wardrobe the way someone browses an art collection — beautifully displayed, searchable by season or designer, with provenance notes. No app has ever given her this experience.

---

**What she wants from an app**

- See her complete wardrobe across all locations, filterable by where she is right now
- Know who has what — a lend tracker for her social circle, without having to text
- A high-value inventory export for insurance purposes
- Know before she buys if she already owns something similar (especially when shopping abroad)
- A record of what she wore to which event — a social outfit history
- A beautiful, editorial-quality gallery view of her collection, organized however she wants: by designer, by season, by acquisition story
- Investment piece tagging — cost paid, current estimated value, whether it's appreciated

---

**What she does NOT want**

- Shopping recommendations (she finds things on her own, always)
- Outfit suggestions from an algorithm
- Anything that looks like a productivity app or a spreadsheet
- "Budget" framing of any kind
- Gamification, streaks, or any language implying she needs to wear things more to justify owning them

---

**Quotes (composite)**

**The feature Sloane unlocks: The Collection View + Lending Circle**

Two capabilities that no competitor has built for this persona:

**Collection View** — an editorial, gallery-quality browse mode that displays the wardrobe as a curated collection rather than a functional closet. Organized by designer, season, or acquisition date. Each item shows: brand, season, where acquired, provenance note if added, estimated current value if tagged. Designed to be browsed for pleasure, not just consulted for decisions. This is the wardrobe as art collection — and it is the feature that makes Sloane's friends immediately want the app.

**Lending Circle** — a simple, private loan tracker integrated into the social trust system. When Sloane marks an item as "on loan to [friend]," NTW logs: who has it, since when, whether she's followed up. An optional gentle nudge ("Zimmermann wrap has been with Mia for 47 days — want to check in?") with language that never makes her feel awkward. This is the borrow/lend feature for a social circle that is too polite to track these things formally — but very much wishes someone would.
