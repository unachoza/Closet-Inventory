Category One

Natural Fibers

Derived directly from plants and animals, natural fibers have clothed humanity for millennia. They are biodegradable, breathable, and often develop character with age.

🐑 Animal Fibers

Protein-based fibers spun from animal coats, cocoons, and hair. They are thermally regulating, naturally moisture-wicking, and extraordinarily durable when cared for correctly.

📍 Protein-based (keratin or fibroin) ♻️ Biodegradable 🌡️ Temperature-regulating
🐑
Animal · Sheep

Merino Wool

Merino sheep · Primarily Australia & New Zealand

The gold standard of wool. Merino's ultra-fine fibers (15–24 microns) make it remarkably soft — it does not itch like coarser wools. Naturally odor-resistant and temperature-regulating.

Semi-Synthetic Fibers

Regenerated cellulose fibers: natural plant pulp is dissolved and re-extruded as fiber. They bridge natural comfort and synthetic consistency.

🌲 Regenerated Cellulose

Starting with wood pulp from trees like eucalyptus, beech, and bamboo, these fibers are chemically processed into smooth, fluid textiles. Sustainability varies dramatically by production method.

Synthetic Fibers

Engineered from petrochemicals, synthetics dominate global fiber production at ~65%. They excel in performance, consistency, and specific applications — but carry environmental trade-offs.

Structure & Construction

Textile Weave Structures

The way threads interlace determines a fabric's drape, strength, texture, and use. Three fundamental structures — plain, twill, and satin — form the foundation of all woven textiles.

Plain Weave

The simplest and oldest weave structure. Each warp thread passes alternately over and under each weft thread — a perfect checkerboard. Produces a flat, firm, and durable fabric with the most interlacing points of any weave, making it the strongest by thread count.

Most interlacing points No right/wrong side Firm & durable Wrinkles easily
Fabrics produced
  • Muslin, Chambray, Chiffon (silk/poly)
  • Organza, Taffeta, Crepe
  • Canvas, Gauze, Voile
  • Most cotton shirting

Compatible fibers: Cotton, linen, silk, polyester, wool — virtually all fibers. The most versatile weave structure.

Twill Weave

Threads pass over two or more threads before going under, with each row offset by one to create a characteristic diagonal rib. This diagonal interlacing distributes tension well, making twill one of the strongest and most durable weave structures. The diagonal also resists dirt and stains.

Diagonal ribbing High durability Drapes well Dirt-resistant
Fabrics produced
  • Denim (3/1 twill) — cotton
  • Gabardine, Chino — cotton/wool
  • Herringbone, Houndstooth — wool
  • Drill, Serge, Tweed

Variants: 2/1, 2/2, 3/1 twill. Herringbone reverses the diagonal. Cavalry twill has a steep double rib.

Satin Weave

Warp threads float over 4 or more weft threads before interlacing, creating long "floats" on the face of the fabric. Minimal interlacing points mean a smooth, lustrous, reflective surface — but also makes satin more prone to snags and less durable than plain or twill. True satin uses warp floats; sateen uses weft floats.

Maximum luster Smooth surface Snag-prone Right/wrong sides
Fabrics produced
  • Satin — silk, polyester
  • Charmeuse — silk (light satin)
  • Sateen — cotton (weft floats)
  • Duchesse — stiff silk satin for bridal

Jacquard Weave

The Jacquard loom (1804) allows each individual warp thread to be controlled independently, enabling virtually unlimited pattern complexity woven directly into the fabric structure. Patterns don't fade, pill, or peel because they are the fabric itself, not printed or embroidered on top.

Unlimited pattern complexity Pattern is structural High cost Durable patterns
Fabrics produced
  • Brocade — silk, cotton, wool
  • Damask — silk, cotton, linen
  • Tapestry — wool, cotton
  • Matelassé (quilted appearance)

Knit Structure

Technically not a weave — knit fabric is formed by interlocking loops of yarn rather than crossing warp and weft. This loop structure allows knit to stretch in multiple directions without special additions. Jersey (single knit), rib, interlock, and fleece are all knit constructions.

Natural 2-way stretch Drapes softly Can unravel (run) No selvedge edge
Fabrics produced
  • Jersey — cotton, wool, synthetic
  • Rib knit — cotton, wool
  • Fleece / French Terry — cotton, poly
  • Performance mesh — polyester

Dobby Weave

A dobby loom attachment allows groups of warp threads to be raised and lowered independently, creating small geometric repeat patterns — diamonds, dots, stripes — directly in the weave. More design capability than plain weave, but simpler and less expensive than Jacquard.

Small geometric patterns Subtle texture Mid-range cost
Fabrics produced
  • Piqué — cotton (polo shirts)
  • Dobby shirting — cotton
  • Waffle cloth, Bird's eye
  • Madras, End-on-end

Pile Weave

Extra yarns are woven into a base fabric and then either left as loops (loop pile) or cut (cut pile). The resulting surface stands up from the base, creating depth, softness, and a luxurious texture. Pile fabric has a directional "nap" — always cut and press pile fabrics in one direction.

Looped or cut Directional nap Plush texture Extra yarn
Fabrics produced
  • Velvet — silk, cotton, rayon
  • Terry cloth / Toweling — cotton
  • Corduroy — cotton, stretch
  • Velour, Plush, Fleece

Leno Weave

Warp yarns are twisted in pairs around each weft thread, locking them in place despite the open gaps between them. This creates an extremely stable, sheer, net-like structure that resists distortion — the openings don't shift or stretch out of shape. Maximum breathability with structural integrity.

Open mesh structure Very breathable Stable / won't distort Sheer
Fabrics produced
  • Gauze bandaging — cotton
  • Cheesecloth, Mosquito netting
  • Marquisette (sheer curtains)
  • Some chiffon variations

Fabric Care

How to Care for Every Fiber

Proper care extends garment life significantly. The most fundamental rule: always check the care label and follow the most delicate fiber in any blend.

Wool, Cashmere & Mohair

Protein fibers. Sensitive to heat, agitation, and alkaline detergents. Never felt these.

🫧
Washing
Hand wash cold or machine delicate. Use wool-specific or pH-neutral detergent (e.g. Eucalan, Woolite).
💨
Drying
Lay flat to dry — never hang (causes stretching). Never tumble dry (causes irreversible felting).
🔥
Ironing
Cool iron with steam, or steam only. Press through a cloth. Never iron directly on cashmere.
📦
Storage
Fold — never hang. Store in breathable cotton bag with cedar. Clean before storing to repel moths.
⚡
Pilling
Use a cashmere comb or fabric shaver gently. Pilling is normal — especially in friction areas.
✨
Pro Tip
Cashmere and mohair don't need frequent washing. Air between wears — hang in a well-ventilated space overnight.

Silk

Protein fiber. Loses up to 20% of strength when wet. Extremely sensitive to heat, sunlight, and alkaline chemicals.

🫧
Washing
Hand wash in cold water with silk-specific detergent. Or dry clean. Never wring — gently squeeze out water.
💨
Drying
Roll in a clean towel to absorb moisture, then hang in shade. Direct sunlight yellows and weakens silk.
🔥
Ironing
Iron slightly damp on the reverse side using a low/silk setting. Steam with care — water spots visible on some silks.
🚫
Never
Never bleach. Never machine wash (unless label says so). Never use deodorant directly against silk — alcohol damages it.

Cotton & Linen

Cellulose fibers. The most forgiving of all natural fibers. Strengthen when wet. Durable and easy-care.

🫧
Washing
Machine wash warm or cold. Cold preserves colors and reduces shrinkage. Linen becomes softer with each wash.
💨
Drying
Tumble dry low or line dry. Remove promptly to reduce wrinkles. Linen wrinkles readily — embrace it or iron damp.
🔥
Ironing
Cotton and linen can take high heat. Iron damp for best results. Linen irons beautifully — press while slightly wet.
⚠️
Shrinkage
Pre-wash before sewing. Cotton can shrink 5–10%. Buy slightly larger if garment is not pre-washed/preshrunk.

Viscose, Rayon, Modal & TENCEL™

Regenerated cellulose. Viscose and standard rayon weaken significantly when wet. Modal and TENCEL™ are much more stable.

🫧
Viscose / Rayon
Hand wash or dry clean. Never wring. Machine washing risks permanent distortion due to wet weakness.
🫧
Modal & TENCEL™
Machine wash cold on gentle cycle. Much more stable wet. Lay flat or hang to dry. Avoid high heat tumble drying.
🔥
Ironing
Low-medium heat. Steam is effective. These fabrics wrinkle easily but respond well to gentle steaming.
✨
Tip
TENCEL™ and Modal are significantly more care-friendly than viscose. Worth the small price premium for ease of care.

Polyester, Nylon & Synthetics

The most care-resilient category. Cold water is the primary rule — high heat can permanently warp synthetic fibers.

🫧
Washing
Machine wash cold, gentle cycle. Most synthetics are very easy care — just avoid hot water, which can warp fibers and set stains.
💨
Drying
Low heat or air dry. Never high heat — can permanently warp polyester. Nylon melts at high temperatures.
🌊
Microplastics
Use a Guppyfriend wash bag to capture microfibers. Wash less frequently to reduce environmental impact.
🚫
Spandex
Never bleach spandex blends — chlorine destroys elastane. Avoid fabric softener — it coats elastic fibers and reduces stretch recovery.
🔥
Ironing
Use low/synthetic setting. Many synthetics don't need ironing. Always use a pressing cloth as synthetics can melt or develop shine.
💡
Odor
Synthetics can trap odors. Add white vinegar to the rinse cycle. Technical sportswear detergents (Nikwax, Sport Wash) help significantly.
